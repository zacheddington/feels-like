    how this number works

    Your skin doesn't feel air temperature — it feels how fast heat is leaving (or entering) your body. So the big number starts with air temperature and adjusts it for the things that actually change that rate. Click any term below to see the exact calculation running on this page, live, with sources.

    Built on dew point, not relative humidity. Relative humidity lies — 100% humidity at 50° is pleasant. Dew point maps directly to how sticky the air feels: above 65° is muggy anywhere on Earth, above 72° is oppressive. The penalty grows steeply as dew point climbs, which is why 91° in Mississippi and 91° in New Mexico get very different numbers here.

    When dew point is low, sweat evaporates instantly and shade genuinely works. Dry heat gets a small credit.

    Direct summer sun can add more than ten degrees of perceived heat, and almost no app accounts for it. We scale this from measured solar radiation, so clouds and low sun angles reduce it naturally. When the sun is part of the number, we also show what shade would buy you.

    At or below 50° we use the NWS wind chill formula, exactly as published. In warm air, moving air helps most when it's dry enough for sweat to work; in between, the two models blend.

    Humid cold air pulls heat out of you faster than dry cold. Raw, drizzly 40° gets marked down.

    The background follows the sun at the place you searched — sunrise gold, pale midday, dusk violet, deep night — with the weather tinting it and the accent tracking the temperature.

    The ledger under the big number shows every adjustment, so you can argue with it. All the constants live in js/feelslike.js.
